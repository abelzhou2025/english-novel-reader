The New Billionaires Behind the AI Data Center Boom

www.bloomberg.com Dylan Sloan, Tom Maloney

Illustration: Glenn Harvey

Wealth

From software to buildings, the race into artificial intelligence has vaulted 16 executives into new stratospheres of wealth.

2025年11月21日 GMT+8 18:00

Share this article

For nearly as long as modern computing has been around, so have the boxy, unassuming data centers that host servers and networking equipment.

But the vast capacity needed for the artificial intelligence boom has resulted in a sudden wave of investment into the once-sleepy industry. And, just like the Klondike gold rush, it's often the companies that provide the picks and shovels -- things like real estate, electricity generation and connectivity -- that are making the most money.

Read more: How to Invest in the AI Data Center Boom

Since January, many of these companies have seen their stock prices surge after announcing splashy deals with major AI developers, even if the details are often fuzzy. But the amount of money pouring into the space and the speed of the run-up in valuations has also raised questions about how sustainable the rally is. A recent selloff in speculative assets has exacerbated concerns that AI-related stocks may be near a peak, with JPMorgan Chase \& Co. Vice Chairman Daniel Pinto warning this week of a looming "correction."

Even as doubt swirls, fortunes are being made. The Bloomberg Billionaires Index calculated the fortunes of 16 founders, investors and executives at eight companies staking out a position in the AI data center market, an area that so far is male-dominated. Here are the people getting rich:

CoreWeave

A failed crypto miner pivots to data centers

New Jersey-based CoreWeave Inc. had an inauspicious start.

Former commodities traders Mike Intrator, Brian Venturo and Brannin McBee quit their jobs at Natsource Asset Management in 2017 to start a crypto mining business. It wasn't glamorous: They bought hardware on Amazon and stored it in Venturo's grandfather's garage. It was also potentially dangerous: The trio feared they'd lit their Lower Manhattan office building on fire when they left their mining rigs on during a particularly hot summer day.

And ultimately, it struggled, leaving them with graphics processing units that would soon be a critical ingredient in the AI boom. The company pivoted to the burgeoning sector, attracting investments from Coatue Management and Nvidia Corp., and becoming a unicorn by 2023.

CoreWeave debuted on the Nasdaq in March at a valuation of $23 billion, making it the biggest US tech IPO in two years. It currently operates more than 33 data centers in the US and Europe, renting out more than 250,000 GPUs.

The firm has been held up as an example of so-called "circular financing" deals in the AI space, but investors don't seem to care. CoreWeave's stock is up nearly 90% since its IPO, vaulting all three co-founders' stakes into the multi-billions. Jack Cogen, their former boss at Natsource, is also a billionaire thanks to an early investment. They've locked in some of those paper gains: Insiders have sold more than $1 billion in stock since a lockup expired in mid-August.

QTS

An early adopter acquired by Blackstone

Years before the AI boom supercharged the industry, Chad Williams laid the groundwork for a data center empire one deal at a time.

Williams, who ran his family's car salvage company as a young man, later built a real estate business from scratch. In 2003, he and his wife Jeannie bought the office of a company that folded after the dot-com boom. The Overland Park, Kansas, building housed a data center, giving Williams a glimpse into the field's potential.

QTS launched in 2005 with the acquisition of another data center in Georgia. Although Williams was early to the game, he kept up his conviction that the bet would pay off. Later purchases included the former Chicago Sun-Times printing plant and what was once a semiconductor fabrication site near Richmond, Virginia.

General Atlantic invested in the business in 2009, and QTS went public four years later. Williams, who's Christian, is known to open some meetings with prayer.

In 2021, Blackstone Inc. took QTS private in a $10 billion deal that saw Williams stay on as CEO. It became one of North America's fastest growing data center landlords. But over time, disagreements bubbled up between Williams and Blackstone. This spring, Williams left QTS after Blackstone bought out his stake, walking away with about $3 billion.

The Kansas billionaire recently announced he would be launching a new company, Quality Infratech Intelligence, to advise data center operators and other power-hungry industrial businesses on their power infrastructure. Over time, it plans to build data centers too.

Nebius

From sanctions to a $19 billion Microsoft deal

Born in the Soviet Union in 1964, Arkady Volozh founded a series of tech businesses before starting software company Yandex in 1997. For years, he navigated a touchy relationship with the Russian state while also growing Yandex's international presence. The company listed its shares in New York in 2011 and grew to a peak valuation of about $30 billion in 2021, making Volozh one of Russia's richest entrepreneurs.

Then Russia invaded Ukraine. Within weeks, foreign investors fled, Nasdaq froze the company's shares and Volozh, who had moved to Tel Aviv several years earlier, was personally sanctioned by the European Union.

He resigned as CEO and spent the next two years fixing his image, issuing a public statement in 2023 that called Russia's actions in Ukraine "barbaric." In 2024, Yandex sold off all its Russian assets for $5.2 billion to a consortium of investors including numerous Kremlin allies. The EU lifted its sanctions on Volozh around the same time.

Following the divesture, the remaining non-Russian Yandex assets rebranded as Nebius Group NV and resumed trading on the Nasdaq. This time around, data centers were the focus: Nebius already owned one in Finland and acted quickly to snap up additional GPU clusters in France, Iceland and the US. It plans to have 100 megawatts of active power by the end of this year.

This September, Nebius' stock soared nearly 50% the day it announced a deal with Microsoft worth up to $19.4 billion.

Astera Labs

A provider of better connectivity for data centers

Early in his career, Sanjay Gajendra became frustrated with working on products that didn't make money. He gravitated toward coworker Jitendra Mohan because he was always winning successful --- and profitable --- patents, Gajendra later said.

The duo were working at Texas Instruments Inc. when they spotted an opportunity: Advancements in AI meant connectivity --- the pathways between clusters of GPUs --- would have to get a lot better.

"A new nervous system would be needed for data centers," Gajendra said in a podcast earlier this year. But Texas Instruments wasn't interested in pursuing the idea.

"We looked at each other and said, 'We see this train coming --- either we let it go, or we take a chance and back ourselves,'" Gajendra recalled. The two engineers founded their company, Astera Labs Inc., in 2017, with the name coming from a shared interest in "all things astronomical."

Within five years, the company that started in Gajendra's garage in Santa Clara, California, had introduced a trio of product lines including hardware and software to manage common data-center bottlenecks. The company went public last year and its valuation has since climbed about four-fold to around $24 billion.

Casey Morrison, a third co-founder, met the pair while at Texas Instruments, where he worked as a systems engineer. Now Astera's chief product officer, the University of Florida graduate's stake in Astera isn't disclosed in filings, and as a result his net worth isn't calculated.

Groq

Specialized chips to run large-language models

Jonathan Ross first began working on computer chip architecture as a side project in 2012, while developing advertising systems at Google. He'd landed the job despite not having a college degree --- or even a high school degree.

Ross was part of a team that came up with the tensor processing unit, or TPU --- a specialized type of chip that's specifically designed for machine learning, unlike the graphics-focused GPUs made by Nvidia and competitors, which were initially developed for gaming.

After incubating the technology internally, Ross and a few colleagues split off from Google and founded Groq in 2016. The next year, Groq secured a $10 million seed investment from venture capitalist Chamath Palihapitiya's fund, Social Capital. Groq became a unicorn in 2021, when it raised $300 million in a funding round led by Tiger Global Management and D1 Capital.

Groq's proprietary chips are less suited to the task of training AI models but they excel at the relatively less resource-intensive process of running those models once they're trained, beating competitors on price and speed.

"When you're running the models, the cost is incredibly important," Ross told Bloomberg TV in May. "Most companies are losing money trying to gain market share."

Groq raised $750 million in a September funding round, which valued the company at $6.9 billion. The round, which initially closed at $600 million but was reopened due to demand, included blue-chip investors including BlackRock Inc. and Cisco Systems Inc.

Fermi

Targeting nuclear-powered data centers

Currently, Fermi Inc. is a data center company without a data center: Its biggest physical asset is a yet-to-be-developed leased site in Amarillo, Texas. But its valuation has rocketed on its plans for a self-sufficient, nuclear-powered compound in the Texas panhandle, named Project Matador.

The company was cofounded by Toby Neugebauer, who led the now defunct "anti-woke" banking startup GloriFi, former Texas Governor Rick Perry and his son Griffin Perry. (Rick Perry, who was energy secretary during Donald Trump's first term, is also a Fermi director and one of its largest individual backers.)

Fermi says its 5,236 acre (2,119 hectare) Amarillo site, which it also calls the "President Donald J. Trump Advanced Energy and Intelligence Campus," will feature four Westinghouse nuclear reactors, which it plans to start bringing online in 2031. In the meantime, it will lean on renewables and natural gas, according to a registration filing.

When the project is finished in 2038, Fermi says it plans to deliver 11 gigawatts of energy powering 15 million square feet (1.4 million square meters) of hyperscale AI computing infrastructure. That equates to data centers representing close to half the size of Central Park powered by five Hoover Dams' worth of energy.

The speed and scale of the proposed buildout are eye-popping, especially for a company that's less than a year old. And the rhetoric is too: Fermi called it "the Manhattan Project of our generation" in a recent shareholder letter. Fermi's shares soared 55% the day of its upsized IPO in early October, and analysts surveyed by Bloomberg unanimously rate the company a buy.

Snowflake

Largest-ever software IPO grabs AI opportunity

Benoit Dageville and Thierry Cruanes had a lot in common when they were working together at Oracle Corp. Both were data specialists, both were French and both had been awarded PhDs in computer science at the University of Paris VI. They also shared a belief that data was going to be an increasingly important resource and its future was in the cloud -- and they agreed that Oracle wasn't the place to capitalize on the opportunity.

"Thierry and I were very good friends working as architects in the core Oracle database team," Dageville said in an interview with ITWire in June. "Two revolutions were happening at that time, and Oracle was not part of them."

Seeing the need for a purpose-built software solution for managing data in the cloud, they left Oracle and formed Snowflake Inc. in 2012. Eight years later, the company went public in the largest software IPO ever with a valuation of more than $33 billion. Its blistering debut made its chief executive officer, Frank Slootman, a billionaire alongside the co-founders; options he was receiving as part of an earlier compensation package were worth $108 million every month. Snowflake's former chief financial officer, Michael Scarpelli, also became a billionaire.

The growth of AI has made the business of organizing and accessing data even more valuable, as large language models require enormous amounts of information to learn. Snowflake is also producing its own AI tools to help customers interact with their data. The company's revenue has grown more than 13-fold since its public debut to $3.6 billion last fiscal year. Shares are up roughly 60% this year, valuing the company at around $85 billion.

Cipher Mining

Bitcoin miner strikes a Google-backed deal

Val Vavilov was 12 years old when the Soviet Union collapsed, leaving his Latvian family in chaos. "I remember how my family and the family of my friends literally lost everything overnight -- all savings, all pensions and all dreams," he said at the World Governments Summit last year.

The experience spurred an early interest in cryptocurrency, with its promises of transparency and decentralization. Vavilov founded Bitcoin company Bitfury in 2011, which became one of the biggest players in the industry.

"I still remember how we motivated ourselves --- we calculated how much money we weren't earning for every second of delay in deployment," Vavilov said in an email to Bloomberg News. "That made every moment count."

As well as mining, Bitfury developed related hardware and software, including purpose-built circuits, servers and a mobile mining rig. Much of that technology can be used in data centers, which Bitfury was also busy building.

"Bitfury has already evolved far beyond Bitcoin mining --- into an AI and high-performance computing infrastructure company that's becoming a global technology incubator," Vavilov said.

Bitfury produced a range of companies, including Nasdaq-listed Cipher Mining Inc., which it spun out in 2021. Like bigger rival CoreWeave, much of Cipher's recent success has been spurred by AI-related deals. In September, it agreed to deliver computing power to AI cloud platform Fluidstack from its data center in Colorado City, Texas. The contract is backed by Google, which received warrants to purchase equity in Cipher.

Cipher's shares are up about 200% this year. Vavilov holds a 12% stake.
