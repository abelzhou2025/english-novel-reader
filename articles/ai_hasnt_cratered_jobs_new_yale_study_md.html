AI Hasn't Cratered Jobs, New Yale Study Says

正在翻译标题...
Regular readers of this column know that I'm something of a "doomer" when it comes to artificial intelligence. It's making it increasingly easy to dupe people online. It's accused of encouraging young people to kill themselves. It may make millions of jobs obsolete.
正在翻译...
Closer to home, it's cratering traffic to news sites, with AI-pocalyptic ramifications for outlets trying to stay afloat.
正在翻译...
So it was with a mix of surprise and something like relief that I read a new study from the Budget Lab at Yale University that argues AI has not led to vast economy-wide negative effects. Yet.
正在翻译...
"Overall, our metrics indicate that the broader labor market has not experienced a discernible disruption since ChatGPT's release 33 months ago, undercutting fears that AI automation is currently eroding the demand for cognitive labor across the economy," the report says.

The report acknowledges "possible AI impacts on specific occupations and sub-populations, such as early career workers." In other words, some specific sectors or types of jobs face more danger from AI than others. It's just too soon to say. The report looks at workplace disruption due to computers and the internet and concludes "even if new AI technologies will go on to impact the labor market as much, or more, dramatically, it is reasonable to expect that widespread effects will take longer than 33 months to materialize."
正在翻译...
In other words, just because the report did not find sweeping effects from AI to date, that doesn't mean that AI won't eventually bring about those kinds of changes. The Budget Lab also noted that the pace of adoption of AI has been inconsistent, even among sectors that are vulnerable to replacement.
正在翻译...
"For instance, generative AI tools were adopted extremely quickly and at mass scale among coders and software developers, who are in the top quintile of exposure," it said. "Meanwhile, adoption has lagged considerably in clerical sectors, despite a similar level of exposure."
正在翻译...
"While anxiety over the effects of AI on today's labor market is widespread, our data suggests it remains largely speculative," the report said. "While generative AI looks likely to join the ranks of transformative, general purpose technologies, it is too soon to tell how disruptive the technology will be to jobs."

That doesn't mean we don't need to worry about AI. Whatever major benefits it will bring -- in addition to the self-entertainment value of letting me generate a video chat background that makes it look like I am toiling in the salami mines -- could be offset by large-scale disruption and job losses.
正在翻译...
"Artificial intelligence is going to replace literally half of all white-collar workers in the U.S.," Ford Motor Chief Executive Jim Farley said in an interview over the summer. "AI will leave a lot of white-collar people behind." Amazon CEO Andy Jassy told employees in June that the company's corporate workforce would be smaller because of "once in a lifetime" AI technology. The CEO of JPMorgan Chase's consumer and community business, Marianne Lake, said in May that the bank's operations staff could fall by 10% over the next few years because of AI adoption. At Anthropic, which produced the large language AI model called Claude, CEO Dario Amodei predicted in a May interview with Axios that AI could wipe out half of all entry-level white-collar jobs and send unemployment soaring to 10-20% in the next one to five years.
正在翻译...
That really brings home the Budget Lab's caution that it's too early to predict what effects AI will have.
